Earlier this week I read Kyle Chayka's latest for <cite>The New Yorker</cite>, called "[The A.I.-Design Aesthetic That’s Taking Over the Internet](https://www.newyorker.com/culture/infinite-scroll/the-ai-design-aesthetic-thats-taking-over-the-internet)." It's about Anthropic's Claude Design and some of the visual giveaways the tool spits out by default. Notably, [Naz Hamid changed the design of his personal site](https://nazhamid.com/journal/fade-to-blue/) and wrote about avoiding the warm terracotta colors and serif type pitfalls, lest there be any question that the design was created, not generated. [Ethan Marcotte also recently shipped a personal site redesign](https://ethanmarcotte.com/wrote/the-house-is-valuable-because-it-is-the-house/) and cited the Chayka article as well in his writeup about the changes.

Not everyone is running away from the vibes. In a recent newsletter, [Joel Dueck wrote about a few people turning to A.I.-assisted coding](https://joeldueck.com/port-watchers/9/) to "liberate" their web content from WordPress. He also announced his own [web publishing tool called Camp](https://joeldueck.com/what-about/camp/), in which the documentation acknowledges the use of Claude Code to help create the software.

I don't usually enjoy reading about A.I., or writing about it. I’m not required to use A.I. at work, so I don’t. I did experiment with the tech a bit in my free time, but the results left me feeling bad... a similar feeling I had the first time I used the "auto-trace" feature in Adobe Illustrator. I have since deleted my OpenAI and Anthropic accounts. Now I am using my free time to [get better at writing JavaScript](https://piccalil.li/javascript-for-everyone) myself. [Figma inserted an "agent button" everywhere on the canvas](https://www.figma.com/blog/the-figma-agent-is-here/) recently and I'm ignoring it. These are some of the boundaries I'm drawing between myself and the technology calling itself "A.I."

What I'm noticing is that everyone seems to be drawing their own boundaries. It's interesting and sometimes surprising to see where everyone has landed. A web designer I admire has a distinct, hand-coded aesthetic but I was suprised to learn in the FAQ of his studio website that they do use LLM tools to aid with more complex JavaScript. Another influential person shared a screenshot of a vibe-coded music player (but not the code behind it) on LinkedIn a few months ago.

I know, [A.I. is dogshit at design right now](https://tomcreighton.com/AI-is-dogshit-at-design/). But maybe the proliferation of A.I.-assisted code and design will lead to more personal software and web projects that would not have existed before. Not all of it will have the smooth and bland default aesthetic, whatever that happens to be according to the model version producing it. Much of it could even be weird (in good ways?). But knowing what was stolen, who exploited, to get us to this point... I have a hard time sitting with it, and I'm abstaining from this flavor of technology, as much as I possibly can. 

I am choosing to see myself as someone who is not competing with A.I. I can't anyway, on either price or so-called "efficiency." I probably didn't want that kind of work anyway. I find myself being more deliberate in design descisions, and writing too, so my work resembles something <em>made</em>, not <em>generated</em>.

I made a couple changes to my own website design, swapping out Hanken Grotesk with [Standard](https://fontsinuse.com/typefaces/159021/standard-wilner), a font that is not on Google Fonts. Standard is based on Akzidenz Grotesk and is quirky and imperfect, with a **B** that feels too wide and a **M** too narrow in comparison. I'm keeping [Dinger](/posts/2025-meet-dinger.html) around for headings, as well as a couple other fonts sprinkled in that This Year's Model wouldn't suggest by default. I also removed the sand-colored background color from my website. I don't have a color scheme in mind to replace it, so I'm sticking to black and white for now, with a dark mode supported by the <a href="https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/CSS/Reference/Values/color_value/light-dark"><code>light-dark()</code> CSS function</a>. More to come.
