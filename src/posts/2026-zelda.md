My beloved dog Zelda died on June 13, after being very sick with liver cancer. Our home feels a lot emptier without her presence. I hadn't been through the grieving process with an animal before, but certainly with other family members. The brain always reminds you you have less time with a pet, but their loss hits profoundly in the same part of the&nbsp;heart. 

Zelda was my first pet, and I didn't know much about dogs before she entered my life. There's plenty I still don't know, but here's what I can tell you about&nbsp;Zelda.

Eleanor and I brought the puppy home on August 1, 2015, one day after our wedding anniversary. We estimate Zelda's birthday around early June. She was born on a farm in Sweetwater, Tennessee. We were told her mom was a Boston Terrier and her dad was a pug/chihuahua mix.  The exactness of this mixed pedigree never mattered much to me—she was cute, and I think she got even cuter (albeit more gremlin-like) as she got older. Zelda had black fur with a white patch on her chest, and a couple white toes. She was bat-eared in appearance, but did not have the smushed face of a Boston or a&nbsp;pug.

Zelda had a healthy appetite, and when she was a puppy she would put both front paws in her bowl to eat. She would often take naps in our laps. Once we came home and found her asleep in the umbrella stand, of all places. Still don’t know how she ended up&nbsp;there.

We thought Zelda was a pretty original name, but we showed up to puppy obedience class at Petco one evening and the only other dog in the class was also named&nbsp;Zelda.

Zelda loved to chase after a frisbee or a ball for exercise. We became dog park people and tried to socialize her early to other dogs and puppies, but she always gravitated toward the humans at the park. Zelda always greeted visitors to the house with a friendly bark and a wag of her tail. Often it would be her entire body wiggling, and if you expressed interest or delight in her, she would be up on her hind legs jumping up for more attention.

When Zelda was excited she would get “the zoomies” and dash around the room or the yard. If you’ve ever seen this behavior in other Boston Terrier dogs, you can imagine how comical it was. She would be curious about a pile of sand, dig a little, go nuts zooming around, and come back and repeat the cycle. Same thing splashing in a stream or shallow&nbsp;pond.

We didn't socialize Zelda around babies or small children at first. When our daughter was born in 2022, we hired a dog trainer to come to our house and observe Zelda’s interactions, and the main takeaway was to reward her with cheese whenever she ignored the baby. But Zelda did get accustomed to our child and soon enough accepted her as one of her&nbsp;humans.

I have fond memories of Zelda accompanying us on trips: a friend's wedding in Birmingham AL; an art show in Providence, RI; Pittsburgh and Rochester; numerous trips between Tennessee and New Mexico, including our move in 2023. I also treasure our favorite walks: Tommy Schumpert Park in Knoxville; the "Ag Campus" at the University of Tennessee; the Knoxville Botanic Garden; The Sandias and our neighborhood parks in Albuquerque.

Zelda entered our lives during a difficult time in adulthood. She was always oblivious to the worries and stressors, but could sense when we needed some comfort. She was with us for many challenging moments in the last 11 years. I am grateful I got to spend as much time with&nbsp;her.

Goodbye, sweet girl.
