When I started reading [<cite>Metropolitans: New York Baseball, Class Struggle, and the People's Team</cite>](https://bookshop.org/p/books/metropolitans-new-york-baseball-class-struggle-and-the-people-s-team-a-m-gittlitz/9579ae52249dc3ed?ean=9781662603006&next=t&aid=21729&listref=meet-the-mets) by A. M. Gittlitz, the New York Mets were in the middle of a losing streak that would reach 12 games. Not the most dismal rcord in the team's history, but notable for one of professional sports' most valuable franchises and highest payrolls today. 

Gitlittz's latest book is a must-read <em>fan history</em> of the Mets, making a persuasive case that the Mets (owned by one of the wealthiest men in the U.S., among many other contradictions) represent the blue-collar, working class spirit of a "people's team".

Through a materialist analysis of New York baseball history, Gittlitz traces the origins of this underdog spirit to the baseball club known as the Metropolitans, a creation of Tammany Hall. These 19th century Mets (and their counterparts the Gothams) played games at the original Polo Grounds in upper Manhattan, eventually forming the National League team known as the Giants. After the formation of the Giants, the rest of the Metropolitan club was sold to an entrepreneur in Staten Island, who ended up trading those best players to the Brooklyn Bridegrooms, or the Dodgers, as they later became known.

Hoping to uncover a hidden working class history of the game of baseball, Gittlitz concedes that the game’s origins were decidedly more professional-middle class. He also acknowledges the average Yankee fan and Met fan may have more in common socioeconmically and even in voting behavior. Nonetheless he gleefully applies this political lens to the history of his favorite team. With Gittlitz's pen, the Dodgers of Brooklyn become “New Deal-coded”, and raven-black jerseys are reminiscent of flags in worker’s movement demonstrations, both in 1889 New York or 1999 Seattle.

Gittlitz paints a vivid picture of the counter-cultural Happenings at the Polo Grounds, the on-field storming and riots at Shea, and the contemporary tailgating and pin-trading scene at Citi Field. From Tom Seaver's stance against the Vietnam war to Dom Smith's wildcat walkout after the 2020 Kenosha shootings, he also documents many activist or solidaristic moments by the players who wore the orange and blue (and sometimes black). No stone is left unturned and no era of Mets history is neglected. Even the [one-time gimmick Mercury Mets](https://www.mlb.com/news/featured/mercury-mets-inside-story) get a brief shout-out in the book.

<cite>Metropolitans</cite> is an entertaining companion to other baseball books I've recently consumed. The book sits comfortably on the shelf with Devin Gordon's <cite>So Many Ways to Lose</cite>, Wayne Coffey's <cite>They Said It Couldn't Be Done</cite> (about the 1969 championship season), Jeff Pearlman's <cite>The Bad Guys Won!</cite> (about the 1986 championship season), and Kevin Baker's <cite>The New York Game</cite> (professional baseball history in NYC up until the end of World War II). 

I don't think you need to be any combination of leftist, Mets fan, or New York partisan to enjoy this book, but it certainly helps. Gittlitz, whose previous book concerns the Trotskyist [J. Posadas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._Posadas) and ufology, includes some gonzo diary entries of the memorable months between March and October 2024 ("The Gay Grimace Mets") at the close of <cite>Metropolitans</cite>. I'm not sure if this lengthy epilogue bolsters the author's arguments, but it does add color and a personal touch. While every season has its highs and lows, the Mets' unexpected run into their own joyful October revolution should stand out in this generation's memories for a long time.

At the time I finished reading <cite>Metropolitans</cite> in May 2026, the team is still at the bottom of the NL East, but they have adopted a [new home run celebration](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZBofNsLijvM): Each slugger is welcomed back into the dugout with a construction worker ensemble in the team's colors and insignia. I'm not sure if the clubhouse has copies of Gitlittz's book lying around, or if this is merely a coincidence:
